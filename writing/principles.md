# Writing Principles: Flesch / Klinkenborg / King

Grounding reference for /writing coaching. When flagging Strong or Weak quotes, cite the
principle name and author in brackets. When setting the next-post experiment, tie it to a
named principle.

---

## Clarity -- Flesch (The Art of Plain Talk)

- **Write as you speak** -- Conversational directness. Formal prose is the enemy. If you would say it plainly, write it plainly.
- **SPO order** -- Subject-Predicate-Object. Keep sentence structure predictable so the reader can track without effort.
- **Short words** -- Common over fancy. One syllable over three. Prefer the word you already know.
- **Short sentences** -- Target ~17 words average. One idea per sentence. Long is fine when it flows; dense never is.
- **Talk about people** -- Use names, "you," "I," "we." Personal references create engagement. Abstract prose feels dead.
- **Verbs over adjectives** -- Verbs carry movement. Adjectives slow things down. When in doubt, cut the adjective.
- **Concrete over abstract** -- Name the specific thing. "A dog" not "an animal." "Monday" not "recently."
- **Pace ideas** -- Leave breathing room between key points. Repetition is not weakness. Readers need processing time.
- **Brevity is not always clarity** -- Dense short sentences can be harder than flowing longer ones. Compress only when it helps the reader.

---

## Sentence craft -- Klinkenborg (Several Short Sentences About Writing)

- **One idea per sentence** -- Split compound sentences by default. Strong long sentences are just strong short ones joined well.
- **Every sentence earns its place** -- Most will need to be killed. The rest need to be fixed. No free passes.
- **Kill transitions** -- Cut "therefore," "whereas," "thus," "but" wherever possible. Trust the reader to follow without herding.
- **No preamble, no postlude** -- Get to the point immediately. End when the point is made. Setup and summary are usually padding.
- **Significance everywhere** -- Do not save the point for the end. Meaning belongs in every sentence, not only the conclusion.
- **Read aloud** -- The ear is smarter than the eye. What sounds wrong is wrong.
- **Delete over patch** -- You cannot fix a bad sentence with the words already in it. Delete and rewrite.
- **Noticing is the work** -- Paying close attention before you write is the writing. If you noticed something, it is important.
- **Cliches are debris** -- Someone else's thinking. They signal you stopped paying attention.
- **Writing attests** -- Trust the thing itself. Good writing shows; it does not argue or persuade.

---

## Voice -- King (On Writing)

- **Kill adverbs** -- "The road to hell is paved with adverbs." Especially in attribution: write "he said," not "he said quickly." Adverbs signal the sentence itself is not working.
- **Active voice** -- Passive is born from timidity. Name who does what.
- **Nouns and verbs carry everything** -- Indispensable. Adjectives and adverbs support; never lead.
- **First word that comes to mind** -- Use it if it is concrete and appropriate. Reaching for a fancier word signals insecurity.
- **Cut 10%** -- Second draft equals first draft minus 10%. No sentence is sacred.
- **Write with the door closed** -- First draft: no input, no second-guessing, full speed.
- **Honest voice** -- Do not sanitize or perform. Write how people actually talk and think.
- **Description: clear seeing first** -- Good description starts with genuine attention, ends with simple vocabulary and fresh imagery.
- **Story before theme** -- Theme emerges from the writing. Imposed theme kills the story.

---

## How to use in coaching

When generating a coaching entry in coaching.md, follow each Strong/Weak quote with
the principle in brackets and the author:

    Strong: "[exact quote]" [Concrete over abstract -- Flesch] -- landed because the image was specific and immediate
    Weak:   "[exact quote]" [Kill adverbs -- King] -- softened the line and made it feel hedged

For Experiment for next post, tie the instruction directly to a named principle:

    Experiment for next post: Apply [Kill transitions -- Klinkenborg] -- cut every "so," "therefore," and "but" that opens a sentence.

If no principle fits naturally, do not invent one. Name the observation directly and skip
the bracket notation.
